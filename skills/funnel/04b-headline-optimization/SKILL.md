# **HEADLINE & SUBHEADLINE OPTIMIZATION SOP**

## **PURPOSE**

Review and rewrite any headline \+ subheadline combination to maximize read-through and conversion, drawing from proven direct response principles and the 100 Greatest Headlines ever written.

---

## **STEP 1 — GATHER INPUTS**

Before writing anything, collect:\_

* Current headline and subheadline  
* Product/offer summary (what it does, who it's for)  
* Avatar research (pains, desires, language they use)  
* Awareness level context (unaware, problem aware, or solution aware?)  
* The CONFIG block from the HTML (for final output)

---

## **STEP 2 — AUDIT THE CURRENT HEADLINE**

Score the existing headline against these criteria. Be brutal.

**The "So What" Test** — If a reader can shrug and scroll past, it fails immediately. A great headline makes indifference impossible.

**The "Who Is This For" Test** — Can the exact target avatar read this and feel *"that's me"*? If it could apply to anyone, it applies to no one.

**The "What Do I Get" Test** — Is there a clear implied benefit or payoff for reading the next line?

**The "Do I Believe It" Test** — Is there a specific detail (number, timeframe, name, mechanism tease) that makes the claim feel real rather than hyped?

**The "Open Loop" Test** — Does the headline create a question in the reader's mind that can only be answered by reading on?

If the current headline fails more than two of these, scrap it entirely and start fresh in Step 3\.

---

## **STEP 3 — CLASSIFY AWARENESS LEVEL DISTRIBUTION**

Every set of 5 headlines must hit all three awareness levels:

**2x Unaware** The reader doesn't yet know they have this problem or that a solution exists. They are living with it, normalizing it. Your headline must create a pattern interrupt — make them see something familiar in a new, unsettling way. Lead with identity, curiosity, a contrarian idea, or social proof that makes them feel left out.

*Patterns that work for unaware:*

* Identity call-out: *"For women who feel invisible after 40..."*  
* Contrarian: *"Why everything you've been told about \[X\] is making it worse"*  
* FOMO/social proof: *"Why 73,000 women have quietly stopped doing this"*  
* Curiosity: *"The one thing doctors stopped recommending — and what they do instead"*

**2x Problem Aware** The reader knows they have the problem. They feel it daily. They've probably tried to fix it and failed. Your headline must validate their pain, name it exactly as they feel it (not clinically), and promise that what they're about to read is different from everything they've already tried.

*Patterns that work for problem aware:*

* Emotion-focused: *"Tired of feeling like your own skin is working against you?"*  
* Question-based: *"Do you ever wonder why nothing you try actually lasts?"*  
* Enemy/conspiracy: *"What the skincare industry doesn't want you to figure out"*  
* Transformation: *"From hiding under layers every day — to not thinking about it at all"*

**1x Solution Aware** The reader has tried solutions — maybe even similar products. They're skeptical but still looking. Your headline must differentiate without naming the product. Focus on the mechanism, the superiority angle, or the specific result others couldn't deliver.

*Patterns that work for solution aware:*

* Mechanism: *"The missing step most treatments skip — and why it matters"*  
* Superiority: *"Why women who've tried everything are switching to this instead"*  
* Time-based: *"Results most people notice in the first 7 days — or it's free"*

---

## **STEP 4 — SELECT 4 HEADLINE TYPES**

Pick the 4 types that best match your avatar's emotional state and awareness spread. One type will be used twice — choose the one with the highest emotional resonance for your avatar.

### **The 12 Headline Types**

**1\. Emotion-Focused (Current Pain)** Speak to the dominant emotion they feel right now — not the problem intellectually, but how it *feels* to live with it. Shame, frustration, resignation, anger. This is the most intimate type.

* *"Tired of waking up and feeling like it's already too late"*  
* *"The quiet embarrassment no one talks about — but almost everyone feels"*

**2\. Social Proof / FOMO** Mass adoption creates safety and urgency simultaneously. The reader thinks: *"If this many people are doing it, maybe I'm the one missing out."* Specificity in numbers is non-negotiable.

* *"Why 60,000+ women have already made this switch"*  
* *"Selling out for the third time — here's why"*

**3\. News / Authority (New Discovery)** Lead with credibility. A new study, a doctor's finding, a surprising research result. This bypasses skepticism because it feels like information, not selling.

* *"New research reveals why most treatments fail within 30 days"*  
* *"Dermatologists are quietly changing what they recommend — here's what they're saying now"*

**4\. Mechanism-Focused (The Missing Piece)** Don't name the product. Name the *concept* of what's been missing. This creates the sensation that the reader is about to learn something they've never been told.

* *"The one step missing from every routine that actually determines results"*  
* *"It's not what you put on your skin — it's what you do first"*

**5\. Transformation / Before-After** Dramatize the journey. Show both ends of the spectrum — where they are now and where they could be. The gap between those two states is the emotional engine.

* *"From covering up every morning to not even thinking about it anymore"*  
* *"She stopped hiding. Here's what changed first."*

**6\. Contrarian / Myth-Busting** Challenge the conventional wisdom they've been following. If they've been doing something "right" that isn't working, this creates a powerful pattern interrupt and a desperate need to know what they've been getting wrong.

* *"Drinking more water won't fix it — and neither will what most people try next"*  
* *"The habit most dermatologists still recommend — that's actually making things worse"*

**7\. Time-Based Promise** Anchor to a specific, believable timeframe. Not "fast results" — *"7 days," "by Thursday," "within the first two weeks."* Specificity makes the promise feel real and creates urgency without false scarcity.

* *"Most women notice a difference within 11 days — without changing anything else"*  
* *"What happens to your skin in the first 7 mornings"*

**8\. Question-Based (Their Burning Question)** Ask the exact question they've been silently asking themselves. When they read it, the reaction should be: *"How did they know I was thinking that?"* This creates instant identification and emotional buy-in.

* *"Have you ever wondered why your skin looks fine in the mirror — but terrible in photos?"*  
* *"Why does it keep coming back, no matter what you do?"*

**9\. Enemy / Conspiracy (External Villain)** Give them someone to blame. This is powerful because it absolves them of responsibility and channels frustration outward. The villain can be an industry, a common practice, a misleading belief — never a person.

* *"The ingredient hiding in most 'gentle' products — that's likely triggering everything"*  
* *"Why the skincare industry has a financial incentive to keep you buying — not healing"*

**10\. Simple Solution (Anti-Complexity)** Counter the "I've tried everything and it's too complicated" belief. Promise that the answer is simpler than they've been led to believe. This works especially well when your avatar is exhausted from over-complicated routines.

* *"You don't need a 12-step routine. You need one thing done right."*  
* *"The embarrassingly simple fix most people overlook completely"*

**11\. Comparison / Superiority** Position against what they've already tried — not by naming competitors but by naming the *category* of failed attempts. Make the reader feel that their previous efforts were missing something fundamental.

* *"Why creams alone will never be enough — and what actually is"*  
* *"Most people try this last. The ones who try it first get results."*

**12\. Identity / Avatar-Specific** Call out the exact person this is for. Age, situation, experience, belief. The more specific, the stronger the self-selection. The right reader leans in; the wrong reader moves on — and that's exactly what you want.

* *"For women over 45 who've accepted that their skin is just 'like this now'"*  
* *"If you've been dealing with this for more than two years, this is for you"*

---

## **STEP 5 — APPLY LENGTH RULES**

**2 of the 5 headlines must be SHORT (3–7 words)** When the headline is short, the subheadline carries the hook forward. These work as a one-two punch — the headline stops them, the subheadline pulls them in.

Examples of short headline \+ subheadline combos:

* Headline: *"She stopped hiding."* → Subheadline: *"And it started with one thing she'd been completely ignoring."*  
* Headline: *"They said it couldn't be fixed."* → Subheadline: *"Turns out, they were looking in entirely the wrong place."*

**3 of the 5 headlines should be 8–12 words** These do more work on their own but still need a subheadline to bridge into the body.

No headline should exceed 12 words. Longer headlines lose the reader before they finish the sentence.

---

## **STEP 6 — WRITE ALL 5 HEADLINES**

Use the 100 Greatest Headlines as your calibration benchmark. Before finalizing each headline, hold it up against the patterns below and ask: *does mine have this quality?*

**Pattern 1 — Specificity signals truth** Vague headlines feel like hype. Specific headlines feel like news.

* Weak: *"A mistake that cost a farmer money"*  
* Strong: *"A little mistake that cost a farmer $3,000 a year"* Apply this: include a number, timeframe, or concrete detail in at least 3 of your 5 headlines.

**Pattern 2 — Curiosity without resolution** The headline teases — it never tells. The reader must read on to close the loop.

* Weak: *"New cream improves skin in 14 days"* (resolved — no reason to read on)  
* Strong: *"How a new kind of clay improved my complexion in 30 minutes"* (what clay? how? read on) Apply this: every headline should end with an open question in the reader's mind.

**Pattern 3 — Self-selection language** Narrow the audience to pull the right reader closer.

* *"To men who want to quit work someday"*  
* *"For the woman who is older than she looks"* Apply this: at least 2 headlines should contain avatar-specific language that makes the wrong reader self-exclude.

**Pattern 4 — Implied story** The reader senses there's a narrative behind the headline and wants to discover it.

* *"They laughed when I sat down at the piano — but when I began to play..."*  
* *"Last Friday... was I scared\! My boss almost fired me\!"* Apply this: 1–2 headlines should hint at a story in progress — something happened, something changed, someone discovered something.

**Pattern 5 — Contrast and surprise** Contradiction creates pattern interrupts. Two things that shouldn't go together — but do.

* *"Often a bridesmaid, never a bride"*  
* *"I lost my bulges... and saved money too"* Apply this: at least 1 headline should juxtapose two unexpected ideas.

---

## **STEP 7 — WRITE SUBHEADLINES**

Every headline gets exactly one subheadline. This is not optional and not a summary — it is a continuation.

**The single purpose of a subheadline: make them read the very next line.**

### **Hard Rules**

**Rule 1 — 1 sentence maximum.** No exceptions. If you need two sentences, your subheadline is doing too much.

**Rule 2 — Never name the product, mechanism, or solution.** Not even obliquely. The moment you resolve the tension, you kill the read.

**Rule 3 — Name the problem only as they feel it** — not clinically, not technically. *"The frustration of seeing the same face every morning"* not *"seborrheic dermatitis symptoms."*

**Rule 4 — Continue the emotional momentum of the headline.** If the headline is surprising, the subheadline deepens the surprise. If the headline is empathetic, the subheadline validates further. Never shift tone between headline and subheadline.

**Rule 5 — Open a new micro-loop.** The subheadline should answer the headline's implied question just enough to raise a new one.

**Rule 6 — The closing-the-page test.** Read the headline \+ subheadline aloud. If you feel satisfied, rewrite the subheadline — it resolved too much. If you feel an itch you need to scratch by reading more, it works.

### **Subheadline Structures That Work**

**The "And Here's Why" Structure** — Subheadline promises an explanation that reframes everything.

* Headline: *"Most women notice a difference in 11 days"*  
* Subheadline: *"And it has nothing to do with what they were putting on their skin before."*

**The "Hidden Factor" Structure** — Hints that there's something they've been missing or not told.

* Headline: *"She stopped hiding."*  
* Subheadline: *"Turns out the thing keeping her stuck had nothing to do with her skin at all."*

**The "Contrast Deepener" Structure** — Takes the contrast from the headline and makes it more personal.

* Headline: *"They said it was just how aging skin works."*  
* Subheadline: *"But the women who refused to accept that answer found something entirely different."*

**The "You're Not Alone \+ But There's More" Structure** — Validates their experience, then promises a reveal.

* Headline: *"Tired of feeling like nothing actually works?"*  
* Subheadline: *"Most women in your position are missing one piece — and it's not what anyone talks about."*

**The "What Happened Next" Structure** — For story-implied headlines, the subheadline teases the next chapter.

* Headline: *"For two years, she covered up every single morning."*  
* Subheadline: *"Then she found out what was actually causing it — and everything changed within weeks."*

---

## **STEP 8 — RANK AND SELECT**

Score all 5 combinations using this rubric. Each criterion scored 1–5.

| Criterion | Question to Ask | Score |
| ----- | ----- | ----- |
| **Specificity** | Is there a concrete detail — number, timeframe, name — that makes it feel real? | /5 |
| **Self-interest** | Is there a clear "what's in it for me" — even implied? | /5 |
| **Curiosity gap** | Does it open a loop that cannot be closed without reading on? | /5 |
| **Avatar match** | Would the exact target person feel "this is written for me"? | /5 |
| **Awareness fit** | Is it pitched at the right awareness level for its intended audience? | /5 |
| **Subheadline pull** | Does the subheadline make closing the page feel like a mistake? | /5 |

**Maximum score: 30\.** The highest scoring combination is your winner. Use this in the CONFIG. List the other 4 as alternatives ranked 2–5.

**Tiebreaker:** If two scores are equal, choose the one that a first-time reader would feel in their chest rather than just their head — emotion always beats logic at the top of the page.

**Non-negotiable condition anchor check (apply before finalising any winner):**

The **headline itself** must name or clearly imply the specific health condition or primary symptom. A cold reader who has never seen this advertorial must be able to identify what body part or problem is being addressed from the headline alone — without reading the subheadline or any body copy.

- **Headline names or strongly implies the condition** → passes
- **Headline is pure angle/emotion with zero condition signal** → **automatic disqualification**, rewrite until the condition is present

The subheadline should then deepen and sharpen the condition reference — but it cannot substitute for headline-level anchoring.

The angle and the condition are not the same thing. "It's just your age" is an angle. "Prostate problems", "nightly trips to the toilet", "joint pain after 60" are conditions. The headline must carry both.

## **QUICK REFERENCE CHEAT SHEET**

**A headline must do at least one of these four things:**

1. Promise a clear benefit (self-interest)  
2. Deliver news or surprising information (curiosity)  
3. Make the reader say "that's me" (identification)  
4. Create a question that demands an answer (open loop)

**A subheadline must do all three of these:**

1. Continue — not summarize — the headline  
2. Deepen the open loop without resolving it  
3. Make the next line feel mandatory

**The 100 Greatest Headlines benchmark check:** Before finalizing, ask: does this headline have the same *quality of pull* as the best headlines on that list? Not the same topic — the same gravitational force. If the answer is no, find what's missing: specificity, story, contrast, or self-interest. Add it. Recheck.
